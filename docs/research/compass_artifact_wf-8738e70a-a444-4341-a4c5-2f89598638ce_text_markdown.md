# A Belgian digital skills radar is buildable — but only if you bypass the public APIs for half your roles

**The radar is feasible as a real-time market pulse, not a data desert — provided you treat public employment services (VDAB, Le Forem, Actiris) as *one of four* ingestion tiers rather than the backbone.** The Belgian labour-market data stack cleanly covers software, data and cloud engineering roles in near real time, but it systematically under-classifies the exact disciplines that define Movify's positioning — product management, UX research, service design, AI strategy, design systems, accessibility. For those roles the radar must be stitched together from ATS public endpoints, public procurement feeds, specialized community boards, and a commercial layer. The good news: that stitched-together architecture is technically tractable at <€500/month operating cost, and there is **no Belgian-native competitor** doing this today — Lightcast and LinkedIn Talent Insights are enterprise-priced and skew to IT/engineering, while Agoria/VDAB/Le Forem publish annual occupation lists rather than real-time skill-level heatmaps. The defensible moat is precisely the non-IT coverage that the question framed as a risk.

## Public employment APIs work for engineers, not for product and design people

VDAB has the most developed API stack of the three Belgian PES: a free, OAuth-secured Vacature API with a "changes since timestamp" endpoint, a free Competent taxonomy API, and a Referentie taxonomy service — all maintained through Synerjob and mapped to ESCO/ISCO. Le Forem is the most open: its **Opendatasoft endpoint `leforem-digitalwallonia.opendatasoft.com` serves real-time JSON with no authentication**, and the Forem team publicly states offers are "mises à jour en temps réel." Actiris is the weak link — per the 2024 Synerjob annual report, Actiris currently has no production vacancy API and pushes only a "limited selection" of Brussels vacancies via SFTP while waiting for VDAB/Forem to integrate fully. Brussels will therefore be structurally under-sampled unless you scrape `panorama.actiris.brussels` and the monthly View.brussels PDFs.

The deeper problem is taxonomy. **Data scientist, cloud engineer, DevOps engineer, web developer and mobile developer are first-class citizens in VDAB Competent and Le Forem ROME**; product manager, service designer, UX researcher and AI strategist are not. UX designer exists as a Competent profile but returns only ~10–40 VDAB hits and gets folded into "Concepteur multimédia" (M180501) in Wallonia. Service designer and AI strategist have no native code in either taxonomy. Competent 2.0, whose successive releases roll out through 2026 (effective date 13 January 2026), adds AI Engineer but still lags on the design and strategy families. Practically, this means any role query hitting the PES APIs for product/UX/service design will pull a **long tail of marketing, industrial design and generic IT titles that must be filtered by keyword regex plus manual enrichment** — a fixable problem, but one that demands its own labelling pipeline.

Market-share evidence is qualitative but consistent. Steunpunt Werk explicitly warns that VDAB's market share of Flemish vacancies is "unknown and variable," and Eurostat JVS confirms Flanders' 4.7% vacancy rate (2024 — highest in the EU) is a survey measurement, not a PES-captured one. Triangulating Agoria's ~20,000+ open ICT posts against VDAB's ~23,000 new monthly vacancies (all occupations) and the total absence of Big-4 consulting brands from VDAB top-vacancy lists, **the best estimate is that PES captures 25–40% of digital-product postings overall but under 20% of senior product/UX/AI-strategy and consulting roles.** LinkedIn dominates the senior and consulting segment with roughly 70–85% coverage; specialised boards add 30–60% overlap.

## Belgium has no equivalent of the UK Design Economy or Nielsen Norman UX survey

The community-level data desert for design, product and AI-strategy is the most striking finding. Flanders DC publishes creative-industry sector sizes (€7.2B, 120k jobs) but no hiring survey; Wallonie Design ran two qualitative "Designer, demain" studies in 2020–21 and then stopped; Design Museum Gent, IxDA Belgium, UX Belgium and Service Design Network Belgium run events and Slack groups but **none publish a recurring quantitative salary or demand survey**. Agoria's BeTheChange and Digital Dashboard track aggregate ICT profiles and cybersecurity separately but do not break out product managers, UX researchers or service designers. AI4Belgium and FARI are policy/research bodies without labour-demand tracking. BECI, Voka and UNIZO produce conjuncture surveys without role-level granularity. The staffing-agency salary guides — Robert Half BE 2026, Hays BE 2026, Michael Page BE, Hudson BE — cover IT, banking, engineering and legal but treat UX/PM/service design as either absent or buried inside "Digital, Marketing & Creative." Even Deloitte's annual Rising Star Monitor and PwC's Bridging the AI Gap 2025 treat talent scarcity at the sector level, not the role family level.

The one unambiguously strong Belgian-specific signal for consulting demand is public procurement. **BOSA e-Procurement (mandatory for contracts >€30k since 2017) plus TED eForms deliver daily, free, CPV-filterable notices** that cover federal, regional (CIRB-2022.023 for Brussels ICT consultancy, Smals frameworks for federal social security, BOSA DG Digital Transformation framework contracts), municipal and hospital/university demand. Filtering on CPV 72xxx (IT services), 79411000 (management consultancy), 72224000 (project management consultancy), 72221000 (business analysis) and 73220000 (development consultancy) produces a real-time feed with 1–3 day lag that is arguably the single highest-quality source for the "enterprises are buying consultancy capacity in X right now" question. TenderWolf and Govex offer commercial layers over the same data (€79–149/month) if ingestion speed matters more than build cost. The caveat is that procurement lags private-sector demand by 3–6 months due to procedural cycles and only covers public buyers — private banks, insurers and telcos still require LinkedIn and ATS ingestion.

## The best MVP stack is four tiers of signal, not a single source

A practical architecture emerges from the research and it looks nothing like "scrape VDAB." The most operationally clean ingestion path combines four tiers:

- **Tier 1 — free, legal, real-time:** Public ATS endpoints (Greenhouse `boards-api.greenhouse.io/v1/boards/{company}/jobs`, Lever, Ashby, Workable) for roughly 100–200 Belgian scale-ups — Collibra, Showpad, Odoo, Teamleader, Silverfin, Deliverect and imec-member companies all publish machine-readable JSON with no ToS issue. Add Le Forem open data, VDAB Vacature API (after partnership), EURES BE feed, and TED + e-Procurement eForms.
- **Tier 2 — low cost:** Meetup GraphQL API (~$200/month Meetup Pro) to capture event topics, sponsor companies and RSVP velocity for ProductTank Belgium, Brussels Data Science Community, Generative AI Belgium, Data Mesh Belgium, DataOps Ghent, BeJUG/BruJUG, Drupal Belgium. Plus niche Belgian feeds: **Creativeskills.be** (design), **datajobsbe** via AI4Belgium's Twitter/LinkedIn (data/AI), **ICTjob.be** (IT) and **Welcome to the Jungle Belgium** via its official Solutions API or Mantiks credits.
- **Tier 3 — higher budget:** LinkedIn Talent Insights (€6–20k/year, KBC Bank is a known Belgian customer) remains the only solution with real-time Belgian skill-level data at scale for senior and consulting roles; Apify scrapers for supplementary WTTJ/LinkedIn coverage.
- **Tier 4 — qualitative overlay:** Devoxx Antwerp sponsor rosters, BetaGroup pitch sponsors, Agoria quarterly vacancy reports (4.38% overall, 4.92% ICT in Q1 2025) and hackeragenda.be for conference/event topic velocity.

Hard legal flags to internalise: **Slack scraping of UX Belgium, SDN Belgium or similar invite-only workspaces violates Slack ToS** and is not viable; LinkedIn scraping at scale remains against ToS despite the hiQ precedent; Meetup forbids non-API scraping; Welcome to the Jungle third-party scrapers technically breach ToS (use the Solutions API). Discord community bots are fine with server admin consent. Procurement data is explicitly open-licensed for commercial reuse.

## Update cadences are good enough for a quarterly pulse, risky for a weekly one

The radar's promised "current + 1–3 month" horizon sits comfortably within what the data layer can deliver, but not all sources update at the same rhythm.

| Source | Refresh | Time-series depth | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **VDAB Vacature API + Arvastat** | Near real-time API; monthly Arvastat | 2007→ monthly, gemeente level | Flemish engineering/data pulse, historical anchors |
| **Le Forem Open Data** | Real-time JSON | Current only (no archive) | Wallonia current postings |
| **Actiris (View.brussels)** | Monthly PDFs, SFTP extract | Multi-year monthly | Brussels context only |
| **TED + BOSA e-Procurement** | Daily, 1–3 day lag | Full archive since 2006 | Consulting demand (public sector) |
| **ATS public endpoints** | Instant | Depends on scraping cadence | Scale-up hiring, product/UX coverage |
| **LinkedIn Talent Insights** | Daily | Multi-year | Premium senior/consulting layer |
| **Agoria vacancy reports** | Quarterly | Multi-year | Macro backbone |
| **Statbel JVS / Steunpunt Werk** | Quarterly | 2010→ | Ground-truth vacancy rate |

The posting-to-appearance lag is not publicly measured in Belgium but consistent with 3–10 days for PES and near-zero for ATS endpoints. The structural issue is not lag but gap: consulting and senior-product roles that never appear on PES at all. This is why the weekly-pulse promise depends on ATS + LinkedIn + TED, not on VDAB.

## The competitive gap is real — and it maps exactly onto Movify's disciplines

No Belgian-native real-time skills demand heatmap exists today. The incumbents sort into four categories that each leave a clear opening:

1. **Belgian public tools** (VDAB Knelpuntberoepen, Le Forem fonctions critiques, View.Brussels, Agoria BeTheChange) are annual occupation lists or long-term forecasters (Agoria's 584k-unfilled-jobs-by-2030 is the classic example). None are real-time, none are skill-level, none integrate the three regions.
2. **Global commercial pulse tools** (Lightcast expanded to 165+ countries including Belgium in May 2025, LinkedIn Talent Insights, Revelio Labs, Draup) have the data but charge enterprise prices (≥€15–30k/year), skew to IT/engineering, and do not segment by the product/UX/service-design taxonomy Movify uses.
3. **Compensation pulse platforms** — **Ravio launched Belgium in 2024** with customers including Showpad, Silverfin, ML6, Robovision and AppTweak, and Figures.hr covers BE via its Mercer partnership — expose real-time hiring and attrition rates as a byproduct of comp benchmarking but are not positioned as skills-demand tools and still under-cover design/product.
4. **Annual staffing-agency salary guides** (Robert Half, Hays, Michael Page, Hudson, Randstad, SD Worx) include demand flags but are PDFs refreshed once a year with minimal product/UX coverage.

The defensible wedges are therefore (i) a real-time tri-regional Belgian heatmap, (ii) skill-level granularity (Figma, LangChain, dbt, Service Blueprinting) rather than occupation-level, (iii) deliberate product/design/AI-strategy coverage that every incumbent skips, (iv) a consulting-skills tracker anchored on TED/e-Procurement (literally empty space), and (v) SMB/consultancy pricing relative to Lightcast and LinkedIn TI. A Movify-built radar that lands even three of those five is unique in the Belgian market.

## Verdict: build it, but not as an API wrapper

The non-IT roles do not create a data desert severe enough to kill the product; they create a **taxonomy and ingestion problem** that determines architecture. A radar that stops at VDAB/Le Forem/Actiris would genuinely fail — those APIs miss 60–80% of the product, UX, service-design and AI-strategy market and lack the codes to classify what they do capture. A radar built on a four-tier stack (ATS endpoints + procurement feeds + niche Belgian boards + Meetup + optional LinkedIn TI) with a keyword-enrichment layer mapping raw titles to Movify's twelve-discipline taxonomy can deliver a credible weekly pulse for engineering, data and cloud roles, a solid monthly pulse for product and design roles, and a differentiating real-time consulting-demand signal from TED/e-Procurement that no competitor currently offers. The realistic MVP scope is "daily-updated dashboard, weekly digest, quarterly deep-dive" — aligned to the reframed 1–3 month horizon. The single biggest execution risk is not data availability; it is the hand-labelled taxonomy enrichment required to make "product manager" and "service designer" searchable against sources that don't natively know those roles exist. That's a one-time classifier investment, not a structural blocker.
