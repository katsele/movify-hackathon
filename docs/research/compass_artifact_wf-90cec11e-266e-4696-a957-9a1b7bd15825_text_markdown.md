# A Belgian skills forecaster is data-feasible — but the edge is in NLP, not datasets

**The data problem is not what will kill this product.** A credible Skills Demand Forecaster for the Belgian IT consultancy market can be built on a stack of free or low-cost Belgian and European public data, supplemented by targeted scraping and one or two strategic partnerships. The real engineering challenge is not data acquisition but skill-tag extraction from messy multilingual free-text (NL/FR/EN), and the real competitive risk is not a lack of data but the fact that **no public source captures the ~50% of IT consultancy vacancies that bypass the public employment services and live on LinkedIn**. For a V1 MVP targeting Movify-scale consultancies (100–500 FTE), public feeds plus one commercial relationship are sufficient to forecast framework-level demand (React vs. Angular, AWS vs. Azure, Python vs. Java) with a 3–6 month horizon. Reaching bleeding-edge granularity (Next.js vs. React, LangChain vs. LlamaIndex, specific LLM stacks) requires either paid commercial augmentation (Lightcast at €30–100K+/yr) or bespoke NLP over scraped job-ad text. Competition in this exact niche is surprisingly thin — TechWolf, the Ghent unicorn everyone thinks about, is actually doing something different (inferring *internal* workforce skills from enterprise tool signals), leaving the *external market demand forecasting for IT services firms* quadrant essentially open.

## The public data backbone is stronger than most people assume

Belgium's three public employment services have quietly become one of the most API-friendly labor-market ecosystems in the EU. **VDAB (Flanders) exposes a full Vacature API with live, skill-tagged vacancies**, plus a Competentiezoeker NLP endpoint that extracts competences from any CV or vacancy text, plus the shared **Competent 2.0 taxonomy** that the four Belgian PES jointly adopted on 13 January 2026. Access is free but the vacancy API requires a partnership approval. **Le Forem (Wallonia) is even more open**: its "Offres d'emploi Forem" dataset sits on an Opendatasoft portal with a full REST API (JSON/CSV/GeoJSON, no key required), including live vacancy text suitable for NLP mining. **Actiris (Brussels) is the weakest link** — mostly dashboards, no vacancy API — but its data also flows into CEDEFOP's pipeline, so it isn't a true blind spot.

On top of these sit three EU assets that matter. **ESCO v1.2.1** (13,890 skill concepts, 1,201 flagged as digital, multilingual in NL/FR/DE, EUPL-licensed, full API and downloadable local copy) is the canonical taxonomy backbone and does distinguish major frameworks like Java, Python, JavaScript, AngularJS, and Vue.js as separate knowledge concepts. Its weakness is **ESCO lags bleeding-edge tools by 1–3 years** — Next.js, specific LLM frameworks, new AWS services are often absent or lumped under generic parents. **Eurostat's experimental `jvs_a_isco3_r1` dataset** goes to ISCO 3-digit × NUTS-1, exposes "Software and applications developers and analysts" (ISCO 251) for Belgium, and is queryable via a free JSON-stat REST API updated twice daily. **CEDEFOP Skills-OVATE** aggregates millions of Online Job Ads classified by ESCO, covers Belgium since 2020, refreshes quarterly — but raw data is locked behind a Tableau UI, so extraction is tedious. Statbel's Quarterly Job Vacancy Survey (Belgium's JVR was 3.50% in Q4 2025, the second-highest in the EU) provides the macro calibration layer.

**Bottom line on public data:** Roughly 70–80% of the forecaster's inputs can be met with free-on-approval sources, updating weekly, at framework-level granularity. This is the MVP foundation.

## Commercial job boards are mostly fortresses, with two real openings

The picture here is uniformly worse than the public-sector one, and this is the central data-strategy question for the product.

**LinkedIn is effectively inaccessible** for a mid-size Belgian consultancy doing commercial forecasting. The Jobs API is partner-only for ATS use cases (posting jobs *to* LinkedIn, not reading market data), Talent Insights requires Recruiter seats at ~€900/month floor with enterprise deals reportedly €50–300K+, and the Economic Graph "Data for Impact" program explicitly excludes for-profit organizations. The Skills Graph (41,000 skills) is not licensable. This creates an **unavoidable blind spot**: LinkedIn is the #1 job-search brand for 33% of Brussels job seekers, and IT consultancy clients recruit heavily there. **Indeed is similarly closed** — its Publisher API is deprecated, and from February 2026 its EU Sponsored Jobs API charges €3 per call and is restricted to recent advertisers. **Indeed Hiring Lab publishes free data but excludes Belgium** from its core country set. **Glassdoor shut API partnerships to new entrants in 2021**.

That leaves three practical routes. **First, scraping** Stepstone.be, Jobat.be, References.be, Monster.be, and VDAB.be via Apify or self-hosted crawlers is technically straightforward at roughly $2–4 per 1,000 listings, and the combined Belgian footprint is 50K–150K unique postings monthly. The legal terrain is grey under the EU DSM Directive Article 4 TDM exception and Belgian database law; survivable if rate-limited and not redistributing raw listings, but genuinely non-zero risk. **Second and most promising, ICTJob.be** — Belgium's pure-play IT board with 933+ active IT jobs, a 130,000+ CV database, and uniquely among Belgian sources a proprietary taxonomy of **250+ technical criteria (IT roles and skills) pre-tagged on every listing**. No public API, but a direct partnership conversation is the single highest-leverage move in this whole data strategy; a likely €10–30K/yr license would dwarf the ROI of Lightcast for a Belgium-specific use case. **Third, Talent.com's Publisher Program** offers a legitimate XML feed across 30M+ jobs in 79 countries for backfill breadth.

On taxonomies, **Lightcast's free Open Skills API** (33,000+ skills, 75,000+ job titles, registration-gated but no payment, commercial use permitted) distinguishes React.js, AngularJS, Vue.js, Node.js as separate IDs and is the practical granularity upgrade over ESCO. Lightcast paid job-posting data for Belgium technically exists — they expanded from 41 to 165+ countries in 2025 — but G2 reviews and the company's own methodology notes show **Belgian depth is thin** (their Belgium coverage leans on CEDEFOP, EU LFS, and Statbel, which you can get for free). **SkyHive (now Cornerstone), Workday Skills Cloud, and Eightfold's ontology are bundled-only** and not independently licensable.

## TechWolf is adjacent, not competitive — and the forecaster niche is open

The most important strategic finding: **the Belgian IT skills demand forecaster niche is not crowded.** The tools people assume are competitors mostly aren't.

**TechWolf** (Ghent, founded 2018 by Andreas De Neve, Jeroen Van Hautte, Mikaël Wornoo; Series B of $42.75M in June 2024 led by Felix Capital with SAP, Workday, ServiceNow, and HSBC co-investing; ~80+ employees) is the elephant in the room, but its product is structurally different. TechWolf infers skills from *internal* digital work signals (Microsoft 365, Jira, Salesforce, SAP, Workday) to tell enterprises what skills their *current workforce* has. It does not forecast external market demand by technology for Belgian IT services firms. Its customers are large enterprises like HSBC, GSK, bp, KBC, Cegeka, Engie, UCB — not consultancies trying to predict client demand. The overlap is the skills taxonomy (TechWolf claims 35,000+ skills, open-source JobBERT model) but the use case is **complementary, not competitive**. A partnership conversation is plausible; TechWolf is unlikely to want to build a consultancy-market forecaster itself.

**Lightcast, Gartner TalentNeuron (now Leeds Equity-owned, merged with HRForecast Oct 2024), Draup, Revelio Labs, Horsefly, and Eightfold** all do external labor-market intelligence, but none is packaged specifically as a "Belgian IT consultancy demand forecaster" — all are global enterprise tools with Belgium included as one of many markets, and Belgian granularity is generally weak. **SkyHive (now Cornerstone) and Faethm (Pearson)** focus on reskilling/automation-risk forecasting, not tactical 3–6 month demand.

On the public side, **Steunpunt Werk (KU Leuven)** is building an integrated labor-market projection model at competence level under the GAP project, due by 2026, with €1M subsidy — relevant to watch but academic/policy-oriented. **CEDEFOP's Skills Forecast 2025** gives Belgium macro projections to 2035. **Agoria's "Be The Change"** projects 541,000 unfilled Belgian vacancies by 2030; their cybersecurity sector data (12.4% vacancy rate, 1,170 unfilled positions of 9,750 FT roles as of October 2025) is granular enough to calibrate against. None of these is a product — they are inputs.

The gap in the market is real: there is no Belgian-specific, tactical (3–6 month), consultancy-oriented, technology-granular demand forecaster today.

## The underrated signals: procurement tenders and hiring-intent surveys

Two data sources are structurally undervalued in most skills-forecasting designs and could become the product's differentiator.

**TED (Tenders Electronic Daily) has a free REST API v3 with anonymous read access, daily XML bulk packages, and a pre-release SPARQL endpoint.** Filter on country code BE and CPV codes 72xxxxxx (IT services), 48xxxxxx (software packages), and the relevant 30xxxxxx/50xxxxxx subsets. Belgium generates roughly 25,000–35,000 notices per year, with IT-related CPV codes representing the low thousands. **The structured fields only give domain-level granularity, but the attached specification documents (bestek/cahier des charges) routinely list specific technologies, certifications, seniority levels, and FTE counts** — a goldmine for NLP extraction. Crucially, Contract Notices give a 3–6 month lead on actual project staffing, and Prior Information Notices can signal 6–12 months out. Belgium's national portal **enot.publicprocurement.be** duplicates above-threshold notices to TED but uniquely captures below-threshold contracts (€30K–€140K range, where SME consultancy work lives) — no API, scraping required, but extremely high signal-to-noise for Belgian-specific demand.

**ManpowerGroup's Employment Outlook Survey (MEOS)** is the best free survey-based leading indicator available: quarterly, Belgian-specific, explicitly 3-month forward-looking hiring intentions, with regional breakdowns. Q1 2024 Net Employment Outlook for Belgian IT was +49%. 74% of Belgian IT employers report talent shortages. This should be a calibration layer, not a primary skill-detail source.

**Google Trends** via `pytrends` with `geo='BE'` (and sub-regions BE-VLG, BE-WAL, BE-BRU) is a legitimate 3–9 month leading indicator for emerging skills — curiosity and learning searches precede hiring demand — at very high skill granularity and zero cost, with the caveat that values are indexed 0–100, not absolute, and small-query noise is real. **GitHub via the `githubarchive` BigQuery dataset** and **Stack Overflow tag velocity** are valuable for bleeding-edge technology detection but the Belgian geographic signal is weak (user-entered free-text location data captures probably under 30% of actual Belgian developers). **Devoxx Belgium and FOSDEM session titles** (both machine-accessible, FOSDEM via Pentabarf XML) are useful 6–9 month topic-mindshare signals. **University STEM enrollment** (Flemish `onderwijs.vlaanderen.be` with API, Walloon ARES less granular; ICT enrollment doubled from 12,000 to 24,000 students 2015–2023) is a 3–5 year supply-side indicator — not useful for 3–6 month demand forecasting.

## What a realistic data stack looks like

A defensible prioritization that delivers a working MVP within one quarter:

| Tier | Source | Role | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** | ESCO API + Lightcast Open Skills | Taxonomy (ESCO for breadth, Lightcast for IT granularity) | Free |
| **Primary vacancy feed** | VDAB Vacature API + Le Forem Opendatasoft API + Competent 2.0 | Live skill-tagged Belgian postings | Free (VDAB needs approval) |
| **Procurement signal** | TED API v3 + enot.publicprocurement.be scrape + NLP on bestek docs | 3–9 month forward demand | Free |
| **Hiring-intent calibration** | ManpowerGroup MEOS, Robert Half Salary Guide, Agoria, Beltug | Quarterly/annual macro validation | Free |
| **Real-time technology signal** | Google Trends (pytrends, geo=BE), Stack Overflow tags, GitHub Archive | Emerging-tech leading edge | Free |
| **Commercial vacancy augmentation** | Scraping Stepstone, Jobat, References + ICTJob.be partnership | Private-sector coverage, pre-tagged IT skills | $2K–30K/yr |
| **Macro ground truth** | Statbel Quarterly JVS + Eurostat JSON-stat API | Denominator / calibration | Free |

The entire Tier 1–5 stack is free. Tier 6 is the only meaningful spend and the only one where the **ICTJob.be partnership conversation is the strategically highest-leverage first call** the team should make.

## What makes this realistic — and where the risks actually are

The data availability verdict is clear: **the project is feasible for a V1 prototype using public and low-cost sources, and credible at framework-level granularity for a V2.** Three risks matter more than the data question:

The first risk is **NLP quality on multilingual free-text**. Belgian job ads and bestek documents mix NL, FR, and EN, often within a single document, and the skill vocabulary evolves faster than ESCO updates. The Competentiezoeker API and TechWolf's open-source JobBERT model are useful starting points, but building a production-grade skill extractor that handles "Kubernetes ervaring" alongside "expérience en React" alongside "AWS Lambda hands-on" is where the real engineering sits — not in data acquisition.

The second risk is the **LinkedIn blind spot**. No compliant commercial route exists for a mid-size consultancy. This means the forecaster will systematically under-represent certain employer types (tech-forward scale-ups, multinationals with centralized recruiting) that skew LinkedIn-only. The mitigation is to be transparent about this in product marketing and augment with proxy signals (GitHub contributor growth, tech-stack shifts on BuiltWith for €295/month+).

The third risk is **that the proposition is validated not by data but by customer willingness to pay**. The Belgian public sector already produces Cedefop Skills Forecast, Steunpunt Werk's upcoming GAP model, Agoria's reports, VDAB shortage lists, and Robert Half salary guides — consultancies have access to a lot of free, if less granular, forecast information. The product's value is not "the data exists" but "we turn it into a weekly, actionable, skill-level, role-level signal tied to your CRM pipeline and historical projects." That integration layer (CRM + historical projects, which are Movify's proprietary assets, not public data) is what makes the product defensible, and that data is already in-house.

## Conclusion

The Belgian IT skills demand forecaster is not blocked by data availability. A working MVP can be assembled from VDAB's API, Le Forem's open data, the TED procurement feed, ESCO and Lightcast Open Skills taxonomies, Google Trends, and ManpowerGroup MEOS — all free, all within reach in weeks. A credible V2 with framework-level granularity needs one commercial relationship (ICTJob.be is the highest-leverage target) and a modest scraping pipeline for Stepstone and Jobat. The one structural blind spot is LinkedIn, and it is unfixable short of an enterprise-tier Recruiter subscription. Competitively the space is open: TechWolf is adjacent (internal skills inference, not external demand forecasting), Lightcast and TalentNeuron treat Belgium as a rounding-error market, and no Belgian-specific tactical forecaster exists. The real moat will not be which datasets you ingest — it will be the quality of multilingual NLP extraction against ESCO/Lightcast taxonomies, and the proprietary fusion of public market signals with Movify's own CRM pipeline and project history. **Build the prototype; the data problem is not the problem.**
